Goal: turn the purely visual CA sketch into an audio-visual one. **A cutout of the running automaton is written into an audio buffer and played back via Web Audio.** Aesthetic: glitch-noise — Ryoji Ikeda, Raster-Noton, alva noto. Not "sonification as melody", but _data as sound material_.

---

## 1. Reference Analysis

### tonemata (kentaro.tools) — CA-based granular noise generator

VST3/AU. Per the product description: wavetables are generated by fast 1D-CA rules and continuously updated; a latency-free **granular engine is coupled at the sample level to the CA clock** and tightly interlocked with the rule set. The display tracks the wavetable state in real time — the audio-visual coupling is explicitly part of the concept, not decoration.

**Transferable:** the CA writes the table, the playback engine reads it — two coupled but separate processes. The CA tick is an _audio-rate clock_, not a frame tick.

### YouTube `azmCM3vBBAQ` — "cellular automata with audio clock precision" (May 2024)

Prototype demo from the same milieu. Core claim of the description: **synchronizing the CA updates with the audio clock reduces the significant (uncontrolled) noise**; further experimentation needed, much untapped potential.

**The central technical insight of this research:** if the CA is stepped on the visual tick (rAF, ~60 Hz, jittering) and copied asynchronously into an audio buffer, uncontrolled discontinuities arise — noise you _cannot shape_. If instead it is stepped **in the audio thread at sample boundaries** (one generation every N samples), the transitions become deterministic: **the glitching turns from bug into parameter.** Consequence for us: the _sounding_ CA instance belongs in the AudioWorklet, not on the main thread.

### Reaktor UL #5241 — "Aliasing Synth"

Esoteric drone/noise classic. Mechanics (per the UL text and an scsynth thread): **an audio table is written and read at the same time — with deliberately "careless", differing speeds.** The errors that result (read/write collisions, aliasing) _are_ the sound. The finest speed changes produce dramatic sonic changes. No MIDI — a noise machine you sample material from.

**Transferable:** a read/write race on the same table as its own sound mode. Trivial (two phasors, one ring buffer), enormous payoff — and it fits, because our CA constantly rewrites the table anyway.

### Ikeda / Raster-Noton — sound vocabulary

Sine tones at the edges of hearing (very high; very low as sub), impulse clicks and click trains, hard noise bursts, **binary gating (on/off without fades)**, extreme dynamics including real silence, hard stereo separation, precision as a statement. Important: the material is _poor_ (sine, click, noise) — the complexity comes from rhythm and switching. Our binary CA delivers exactly that: 0/1 transitions are clicks, rows are pulse trains, densities are gates.

### Synthesis

|              | tonemata                                      | Audio-Clock Video             | Aliasing Synth                    |
| ------------ | --------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| CA role      | writes wavetable                              | stepped sample-synchronously  | (no CA — race as error source)    |
| Sound source | granular over CA table                        | CA state directly             | read/write collisions             |
| Clock domain | audio (sample-locked)                         | audio                         | audio                             |
| Lesson       | separate table + engine, make table visible   | put the CA in the worklet     | build in a race mode              |

**All three operate entirely in the audio-clock domain. None transfers visual frames into audio.** That is the single most important finding — and the main deviation from the naive approach ("copy a canvas cutout into a buffer every frame").

## 2. Sonification Design Space

The frame (cutout → buffer → playback) is right. Four viable mappings, in ascending order of effort:

### A. Region scan as wavetable (base)

A cutout (variable size — see §5; worked here at 64×64) is written row by row (raster scan) into a table: cell 1 → `+g`, cell 0 → `−g`. A phasor reads it as a loop.

The math: 64×64 = 4096 samples @ 48 kHz → at rate 1× an **~11.7 Hz loop** = rhythmic clicking/rattling (very Ikeda). Higher rates pitch into the tonal range; the **row periodicity (64 samples) produces a carrier at ~750 Hz** with comb structure. Our plaid patterns are quasi-periodic → harmonic combs with glitching breaks at the weave edges. The CA's progress rewrites the table → spectral animation.

### B. Read/write race (aliasing mode)

Like A, but a ring buffer: a write phasor writes the region scan at rate `W`, a read phasor reads at rate `R ≠ W`. Collisions = Aliasing-Synth behaviour. **The `W/R` ratio ("drift") is the main performance parameter** — the reference says the finest movements around 1.0 = dramatic changes, so very fine slider resolution there. Extra cost over A: minimal.

### C. Audio-clock CA (precision mode — the tonemata/video insight)

The worklet runs its **own CA instance** (a copy of the cutout including the rule), stepped **every N samples**. `N` = "samples per generation" becomes a sound parameter:

- N large (≥ 2048) → slow texture morphs
- N small (64–256) → **the CA _is_ the oscillator, the rule becomes audible**

Budget: 64×64 = 4096 cells; at N=64 → 4096 × 750 = ~3 M cell updates/s. Our kernel runs at ~0.25 ns/cell → uncritical. For comparison: the **600×600 full grid is not audio-rate capable** (~4 ms/step). So the cutout is not only a UX decision but also a performance one.

In practice: our `CA` class is already parameterized and dependency-light — it runs in the worklet once the `@thi.ng/pixel` part (rendering only) is split off. **TODO on implementation:** extract rendering from `CA` or introduce a constructor flag `headless`, so the worklet allocates no IntBuffer.

### D. Event layer (Ikeda layer)

In addition to the table playback: cell _events_ are switched directly — births → 1-sample impulses (clicks), live density → gate/amplitude of a sub sine, edge density → noise share. Cheap (falls out of the CA step anyway) and delivers the **dynamics layer (silence ↔ burst)** that pure wavetable scanning lacks.

### Rejected

- **FFT mappings** (region as spectrum + iFFT) — sonically washed out, contradicts the hard binary aesthetic.
- **MIDI/note mappings** — wrong genre.

## 3. The biggest sonic risk

**Static periods.** Our curated keeper rules converge into quasi-stationary weaves (which is exactly why they look good). As a pure wavetable that means: **after settling, a standing harmonic tone instead of glitch.**

The countermeasures are exactly B (race drift), C (small N) and D (event layer). Therefore: **B belongs in the MVP, not in phase 2.** An MVP with only A would be a pretty disappointment.

Further risks:

- **Loudness accidents** — binary full-scale tables are brutal. Limiter + conservative default (−18 dB) + gain ramps are not optional.
- **DC offset** — a mostly "alive" region is almost pure DC. An HPF is mandatory (see below).
- **Safari** — Blob worklet + AudioContext quirks. An explicit test point, not an assumption.

## 4. Technical Architecture

### AudioWorkletProcessor, not ScriptProcessorNode

(The latter is deprecated and runs on the main thread → exactly the jitter the references eliminate.)

Signal chain **in the worklet**:

```text
table read → DC blocker (one-pole HPF ~20 Hz) → soft clip → gain ramp (~10 ms, click-free)
```

Native graph: `workletNode → destination`, optionally a `DynamicsCompressorNode` as a limiter safety net.

**Never allocate in `process()`.** Double-buffer in the worklet, swap on message receipt.

### Two hard platform constraints (they determine the design)

**1. SharedArrayBuffer is out.** SAB needs cross-origin isolation (COOP `same-origin` + COEP `require-corp`), i.e. response headers — which a single-file HTML opened via `file://` or hosted without headers cannot set. **Consequence:** transport via `port.postMessage` with **transferable ArrayBuffers** (zero-copy ownership transfer).

Budget: 64×64 = 16 KB per visual generation @ 60 Hz ≈ 1 MB/s → uncritical.

_(On GitHub Pages COOP/COEP couldn't be set either — Pages allows no custom headers. So the design has to work without SAB regardless.)_

**2. Worklet module loading in the single-file build.** `audioWorklet.addModule(url)` needs a URL. Solution: bundle the processor source inline as a `?raw` import, then

```ts
const blob = new Blob([processorSrc], { type: "text/javascript" });
await ctx.audioWorklet.addModule(URL.createObjectURL(blob));
```

Established in Chromium/Firefox; **Safari has historically had quirks here → verification list**, fallback `data:` URL. This keeps `build:single` a single HTML file.

### Clock domains — phase change

- **MVP (A/B):** the CA stays visually clocked (rAF), the worklet consumes table updates asynchronously. This _does_ have the jitter the video described — but in scan/race mode the race collisions dominate sonically anyway, and the jitter is absorbed into the texture.
- **Phase 2 (C):** the sounding CA runs in the worklet at sample boundaries. The main thread only sends seeds ("capture region"), rule changes, parameters.

Then **two CA instances** exist — the visual and the sounding one. This is **deliberately not a bug**: the reference tools separate them the same way. Perfect continuous congruence of image and sound is fundamentally incompatible with audio-clock precision; **moments of congruence (capture) are the interaction model.**

### Performance budget (128-frame quantum @ 48 kHz = 2.7 ms)

|                              | Cost                                               |
| ---------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| 128 table reads + HPF + clip | trivial                                            |
| Race mode: +128 writes       | trivial                                            |
| Audio-clock CA 64×64 @ N=64  | 2 steps/quantum × 4096 cells ≈ 8k ops → **< 1 %**  |

There is headroom for polyphony (multiple regions).

## 5. GUI Integration

### Region selection on the canvas

A visible **selection frame** in the existing blue (`#3b3bff`) over the full 600×600 grid. Size is **freely resizable**: **Shift-drag rubber-bands an arbitrary W×H rectangle** (irregular window, min 1×1 cell), and **square-size presets (16 / 32 / 64 / 128)** in the panel are a shortcut that sets `regionW = regionH`; a Shift-drag rectangle un-presses the preset buttons. Default is 32×32. The buffer length is `regionW · regionH`, which sets the loop pitch (`state.ts:56`) — so it changes continuously while dragging.

**Interaction (important):** a plain drag draws/erases as before; **Alt-drag glides the region center**, **Shift-drag sizes the region box**. The frame exists only while audio is active — **without audio the sketch stays exactly as before.**

### Panel section "Audio"

New, below the pencil block, **collapsible** (details pattern), so the panel doesn't grow without audio:

- **Power toggle** (▸ Audio) — starts/resumes the AudioContext. _Autoplay policy: it requires a user gesture — the toggle **is** the gesture._
- **Volume** — conservative default (−18 dB)
- **Mode** — segmented "Scan / Race" (analogous to the existing Draw/Erase pattern)
- **Rate** — logarithmic, ±3 octaves around 1×
- **Drift** (race only) — W/R ratio, **very fine resolution around 1.0**
- **Region Size** — square presets 16 / 32 / 64 / 128 (plus free Shift-drag resize on the canvas)
- **Level meter** — a thin strip

Plus a **waveform strip** (mini canvas ~248×40 px): draws the _sounding_ table. That is the tonemata lesson — the audio-visual coupling is not just asserted but shown. The worklet posts decimated snapshots back (~15 Hz is enough).

Keyboard: **`a` = audio on/off.** Existing shortcuts unchanged.

### State

Audio parameters as their own slice in the `defAtom` — and, like `brush`/`tool`, **NOT part of the reset defaults** (reset concerns the simulation, not the instrument).

**The `gen$`/`fps$` principle still holds:** high-frequency meter/waveform data runs past the atom as streams.

**And don't forget the trap:** every new atom reaction needs `dedupe(equiv)`, not `dedupe()`.

## 6. Phase Plan

### Phase 1 — MVP "Scan + Race"

Region frame (default 32×32, Alt-drag to move, Shift-drag to resize freely, square presets 16/32/64/128) · worklet with table and two phasors · Scan/Race modes · transport via transferables · DC block + limiter + ramps · panel section (Power/Volume/Mode/Rate/Drift/Size) · waveform strip · key `a`.

→ The core experience is complete: _the cutout sounds, and drift breaks it in the good way._

### Phase 2 — Audio-clock CA

CA instance into the worklet (`headless` variant) · "samples per generation" as a parameter · capture button. Brings the precision aesthetic of the references and **solves the static risk structurally.**

### Phase 3 — Ikeda layer & capture

Event clicks · sub gate · noise layer with mixer · **WAV recording** (worklet capture → download, analogous to the existing download-PNG pattern) · optionally a second region = a second voice, hard-panned.

Each phase is individually shippable.

## 7. Open questions for the first prototype

A usability check after phase 1 — five minutes of playing it yourself, three questions:

1. Do you find the frame?
2. Are the Alt-drag (move) / Shift-drag (resize) modifiers discoverable, or do they need the on-canvas hint?
3. Do you understand Scan vs. Race without docs?

Open sonically: whether the keeper rules are _interesting enough_ as a wavetable, or whether audio needs its **own rule pool** (the band/flood rules rejected for visuals could be sonically attractive — dense flood = noise bed, bands = strong periodicity). **Don't rule it out prematurely: the visual filter is not an audio filter.**
